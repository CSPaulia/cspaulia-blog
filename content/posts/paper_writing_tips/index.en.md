### Citation Format

Common IEEE reference format:

- Journal: [1] Author, “Title,” *Journal Name Abbreviation*, vol. volume, no. issue, pp. start-end page, month, year, doi: ...
- Conference: [2] Author, “Title,” in *Proc. Conference Name*, Location, year, pp. start-end page.
- Book: [3] Author, *Title*, edition. Place of publication: Publisher, year.
- Web: [4] Organization or Author, “Title,” accessed date: year-month-day. [Online]. Available: URL

## Equations

Refer to Nature's equation writing standards:

- **Scalar variables** and **constants** should be italicized;
  e.g., $x$, $\alpha$, $\pi$.
- **Vectors** and **matrices** should be bold and not italicized;
  e.g., $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{A}$.
- Subscripts and superscripts are typically set in roman type, and only italicized when they represent variables;
  e.g., $x_{\mathrm{max}}$, $y_{\mathrm{pred}}$; if a subscript denotes a variable, it can be written as $x_i$.
- Unit dimensions are recommended to be expressed using negative exponent form, e.g., kg m⁻¹ s⁻², rather than kg/ms²; the word “per” can also be used to express division between units;
  e.g., $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, $\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$, or written as “m per s”.
- As part of the main text, equations should end with punctuation such as a period, comma, or semicolon, unless immediately followed by a bracket or other symbol;
  e.g., $E=mc^2$.
